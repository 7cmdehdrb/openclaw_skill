Abstract

1

Viewpoint control is a critical challenge in VR-based telemanipulation, especially in highdensity logistics environments where stable line-of-sight is essential. Conventional gesturebased methods, such as the fist-based approach, often suffer from posture-dependent
anisotropy due to anatomical wrist constraints, leading to trajectory distortion. Furthermore, gaze-coupled methods limit seated accessibility, as users must physically strain to
achieve downward or backward views. This study proposes a flight-like VR locomotion
framework utilizing an MLP-based fingertip gesture recognition model and decoupled
6-DOF velocity mapping. By anchoring the navigation vector to the HMD-frame origin, the
method achieves posture-invariant control isotropy and 360° seated accessibility. Experimental results indicate improved tracking precision, particularly during significant joint
articulation. Circular path evaluations showed a 21.89% reduction in RMSE and a 53.86%
reduction in maximum trajectory error compared to the fist-based method. In complex sequential navigation tasks, the proposed system demonstrated enhanced stability, recording
a Trajectory RMSE of 0.18. Additionally, the decoupled scheme achieved a 93% success
rate for extreme viewing angles, providing high accessibility where gaze-coupled methods
faced ergonomic constraints. These findings suggest the decoupled fingertip-pointing interface is a robust, ergonomic alternative for industrial teleoperation in confined virtual
environments.

As manufacturing automation and smart factories continue to advance, the importance
of efficient material-handling systems that connect production processes and logistics hubs
is increasingly emphasized.[1–4] In this context, teleoperation technologies utilizing virtual
reality (VR) are receiving attention as an approach that enables operators to intuitively
control robots even in complex industrial environments. However, in logistics environments where cargo is densely stored in confined spaces, automated pick-and-place tasks
performed by robots impose various physical constraints.[5] In such situations, depending
on the viewpoint, visual occlusion may occur in which the target object or the robot gripper
is obscured, and this acts as a factor that degrades task accuracy and success rate.[6–8]
In particular, in environments that require precise grasping operations, such occlusion
interferes with the alignment between the robot end effector and the target object, thereby
reducing operational efficiency.[9] Therefore, to overcome these limitations, tasks must be

VR locomotion methods are categorized into roomscale-based, teleportation-based,
controller-based, and motion-based methods according to the interaction modality, and
each approach exhibits distinct technical characteristics under the physical constraints
of high-density logistics environments.[11] First, roomscale methods restrict the range of
virtual movement to the actual physical space occupied by the operator, leading to a spatial
mismatch issue in logistics sites where large-scale shelf structures must be explored.[12,13]
Teleportation-based methods support instantaneous relocation through destination targeting. However, the abrupt change in viewpoint degrades the operator’s spatial awareness
and limits the fine adjustments needed for precise alignment of suction grippers.[14–17]
Furthermore, while controller-based methods provide continuous movement through
devices such as joysticks, they necessitate the possession of separate hardware. This complicates teleoperation tasks, where robot control and viewpoint manipulation must be
performed simultaneously.[18,19] Conversely, motion-based methods utilizing bodily actions are considered alternatives as they allow for intuitive control without additional
devices. Nonetheless, commonly studied techniques such as walk-in-place or arm swinging
require an excessive range of motion. These methods not only induce physical fatigue
during sustained operations but also cause upper-body swaying, which acts as physical
interference, hindering the precise manipulation of the robot’s end effector. Therefore,
to maintain the stability of precise manipulation, there is a requirement for an interface
design that minimizes the range of body motion while supporting continuous viewpoint
control.[20,21]

To ensure the stability of precision manipulation, several studies have explored singlehand gesture-based viewpoint control utilizing Head-Mounted Display (HMD)-embedded
cameras.[22–25] A representative approach is the fist-based method[22,23], which maps
the wrist’s orientation to viewpoint rotation and translation. While this method supports
continuous velocity control, it inherently suffers from a lack of control isotropy due to the
anatomical constraints of the human wrist joint. Achieving a full 3 degree-of-freedom (DOF)
motion without rotating the forearm is physically limited, making the reachable control
workspace highly dependent on the initial reference frame. For instance, if the gesture

begins with a specific hand pose such as the back of the hand facing the 9 o’clock direction
the movement becomes severely distorted or physically blocked in certain sectors, notably
the 4–8 o’clock range. Although changing the starting posture may shift this restricted zone
(e.g., shifting the dead zone to 4–8 o’clock), the fundamental issue of a constrained forearm
axis remains. Consequently, users are often forced to reset their hand pose frequently to
maintain a fixed-forward forearm alignment, disrupting control continuity. Furthermore,
the fist gesture is vulnerable to self-occlusion by the forearm, leading to low occlusion
robustness and potential recognition failures during downward-aiming maneuvers.
Alternatively, a pointing-based method[24,25] has been proposed, where the viewpoint’s translation is determined by the direction of the index finger. In this framework,
the viewpoint orientation is strictly gaze-coupled, meaning it is dictated by the HMD’s
orientation. While this approach offers high intuitiveness, it presents significant ergonomic
challenges, limiting seated accessibility. Since the pose is coupled with the head direction,
users must physically rotate their entire body or strain their necks to navigate toward
backward or top-down views. Moreover, these systems often rely on discrete velocity
control, which limits the operational efficiency for tasks requiring fine-tuned, high-speed
navigation in expansive environments.
To overcome these limitations specifically the posture-dependent control anisotropy,
ergonomic constraints of gaze-coupling, and low occlusion robustness this study proposes a
velocity-based flight-like VR locomotion method. Our approach utilizes a fingertip pointing
gesture to simultaneously control both linear and angular velocities, enabling seamless
6-DOF viewpoint maneuvering without explicit mode switching. The core research contributions of this paper are as follows:

•

•

Enhanced Control Isotropy via Pointing-based Mapping: By utilizing a fingertipbased mapping instead of a wrist-posture-dependent method, we eliminate the trajectory distortion caused by anatomical joint limits, ensuring uniform control sensitivity
across all directions regardless of the initial posture.
Seated-Position Full-Accessibility via Decoupled Control: By decoupling the viewpoint orientation from the user’s head gaze, our system enables intuitive navigation
toward downward or backward views from a seated position, removing the physical
rotation constraints of the body.
Robust Continuous Velocity Control: We introduce an anchor-based reference frame
that allows for continuous velocity modulation. This method ensures high occlusion
robustness by maintaining a clear LoS to the fingertip and supports both rapid travel
and stabilized fine-tuning.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 describes the proposed
flight-like VR locomotion framework, including the Multi-Layer Perceptron(MLP)-based
gesture recognition and the velocity-mapping algorithm. Section 3 presents the experimental setup and methodology, including the evaluation of control isotropy on a Head-Forward
Plane (HFP) and sequential navigation in complex logistics environments. Section 4 analyzes the experimental results, focusing on isotropic maneuverability and task efficiency
compared to conventional methods.

2.1. MLP-based Locomotion Triggering Module

125

For the user hand gesture recognition required in this study, hand skeleton data
acquired through the Oculus Integration Software Development Kit(SDK)[26]. The hand
skeleton data consists of 24 joints, and each joint contains information on its spatial position
vector(x, y, z) and rotation represented as quaternion(qx, qy, qz, qw). The joints are composed
of the major parts of the human hand: the forearm, wrist, thumb, index, middle, ring, and
pinky; the thumb and pinky are further subdivided into 5 joints each, while the index,
middle, and ring fingers are subdivided into 4 joints each. The thumb comprises the
trapezium, metacarpal, proximal, distal, and tip, while the pinky consists of the metacarpal,
proximal, intermediate, distal, and tip. Additionally, the index, middle, and ring fingers are
divided into the proximal, intermediate, distal, and tip, respectively. The hand skeleton data,
represented in the wrist reference frame {W }, is split into a training set and a validation
set at a 8:2 ratio for training the hand gesture recognition model. The hand-skeleton data
were acquired from continuous movements, and a total of 4,312 data points were used for
training. The training set was used to update the model’s weights during training, while
the validation set was used to prevent overfitting and to determine the timing of early
stopping.
The input for hand gesture recognition consists of skeleton data, comprising the positions and orientations of hand joints, in which joint-level geometric structures and relative
relationships serve as the primary discriminative features. Zhang et al.[27] demonstrated
that, in skeleton-based action recognition, MLP-based architectures that learn point-wise
joint features can achieve sufficient representational capacity without relying on complex

involves lower-dimensional inputs and a limited number of gesture classes, making these

on these considerations, an MLP-based classification model was adopted in this study.
Since the architecture and training performance of an MLP model can vary significantly
with hyperparameter configuration, a hyperparameter search was conducted over the
number of hidden layers, the number of nodes per layer, the batch size, the number of
epochs, and the early stopping patience. As a result, the final model was configured
with two hidden layers consisting of 32 and 128 nodes, respectively, while the trainingrelated hyperparameters were set to a batch size of 32, 50 training epochs, and an early
stopping patience of 25. The trained model achieved an accuracy of 99.8% on a test dataset
consisting of 666 samples that were not used during training. However, since frame-wise
classification results may lead to occasional misclassifications, a sliding window–based postprocessing method was applied to ensure recognition continuity and stability. Specifically,
the recognized gesture is maintained when a majority of frames within a predefined
temporal window are classified as the same gesture, effectively suppressing discontinuous
recognition results. The resulting stabilized gesture recognition outputs are used as input
commands for viewpoint control in the proposed VR locomotion method.
2.2. Relative Spatial Path Generation Module
Once the operator’s locomotion intent is identified, the system utilizes gaze information and pointing coordinates as geometric constraints to generate a curved path for optimal
viewpoint transitions in complex 3D environments. The trajectory follows a quadratic Bezier
curve that smoothly connects the user’s current position to the destination. The three points
defining the curve’s geometry are established as follows: The starting point (P0 ) is defined
as the operator’s current HMD position. The destination (P2 ) is intuitively specified in the
virtual environment using the 3D coordinates of the operator’s index fingertip during the
pointing gesture. Finally, the intermediate control point (P1 ) dictates the initial steering and
curvature of the trajectory; it is positioned at an offset distance d (0.3m) along the current
gaze vector (d⃗curr ), as illustrated in Figure 2.

2.3. Multi-axis Velocity Vector Mapping Module
This section describes the control algorithm that smoothly transitions the user’s viewpoint along the generated Bezier trajectory. The system calculates linear and angular
velocities in real-time by analyzing the geometric properties of the path and the user’s
gesture state. The linear velocity (vlin ) is determined based on the distance between the
current HMD position (Phmd ) and the target destination (Ptip ). To prevent cybersickness
caused by excessive speed in the virtual environment (VE), the maximum velocity (Vmax ) is
set to 0.597 m/s [29], and the final velocity is determined by multiplying Vmax by a distance
coefficient (σ). The coefficient σ is a normalized value of the distance between the HMD
and the destination within a predefined range (dmin , dmax ). Specifically, dmin is set to 0.2 m
as the recognition threshold of the VR interface, and dmax is set to 0.5 m, corresponding to
the average arm length of an adult female, to allow for maximum speed within a natural

Steering control is designed to progressively align the virtual camera’s forward vector
with the tangent direction of the generated path. The rotational speed is calculated based
on the orientation error between the current forward vector (dˆcurr ) and the target forward

these forward vectors. To suppress unintended rotations caused by sensor noise or hand

the rotational speed is set to zero, effectively filtering high-frequency oscillations during
linear movement. Ultimately, the rotational speed ω is defined as a proportional function
of the angular error.

2.4. VR Interface of Proposed System

230

To enhance user intuitiveness and convey the system’s control state in real time, visual
feedback elements were implemented. Through visual guides, the system supports users in
predicting the movement trajectory and maintaining stable gesture input, and the overall
configuration is illustrated in Figure 3. At the center of the user’s field of view, a gaze center
dot is displayed to indicate the viewing direction. This marker visualizes the endpoint of
the gaze vector, which serves as the geometric basis for computing the intermediate control
point P1 defined in Section 2.2. Through this center dot, the operator can observe in real
time how their gaze direction contributes to steering along the generated trajectory and
can use it as an aiming reference for viewpoint navigation. To ensure continuity of hand
tracking, an interaction box that visualizes the recommended manipulation region is also
provided. This guide box is designed based on the tracking system’s effective sensing range
and encourages the user to keep the non-dominant hand within the detectable region. As a
result, interruptions in gesture recognition caused by the hand leaving the field of view
during locomotion control are effectively prevented.
The curved trajectory is rendered in real time in the virtual environment as a spline.
This spline provides feedback not only on the intended movement direction but also on the
geometric path determined by the relative relationship between the gaze direction and the
index fingertip position P2 . The user can actively modulate the curvature of the trajectory
by adjusting the hand position laterally while keeping the head fixed, or by moving the
gaze while keeping the hand stationary. The visualized spline continuously reflects the
resulting coordination, allowing the user to intuitively anticipate the required maneuvering
effort when navigating around obstacles or approaching a target from a specific angle.
Finally, a velocity arrow is displayed to visualize the motion command determined by the
proposed locomotion method. This arrow represents the final velocity vector computed
from the generated trajectory, encoding both its direction and magnitude. By providing
immediate visual feedback on the resulting velocity, the velocity arrow enables the user to
understand how their pointing gesture and gaze input are translated into actual motion,
thereby improving controllability and predictability of viewpoint navigation.

3.1. Control Isotropy Identification via Circular-path Tracking

260

The primary objective of this experiment is to evaluate the control isotropy of the proposed fingertip-based method in comparison to the fist-based method. Control isotropy was
assessed by comparing the geometric fidelity of trajectories generated on the HFP, in which
users were tasked with executing a perfectly circular path of constant curvature. While
the fist-based method uses wrist angles for directional control, it is fundamentally limited
by the wrist’s anatomical range of motion, resulting in a non-uniform control workspace.
Specifically, when the back of the hand faces the 9 o’clock direction, the trajectory exhibits
significant skewness or anatomical singularities in the 4–7 o’clock sector. Because this
restricted zone shifts dynamically with the initial posture, the fist-based method fails to
provide a consistent steering response across a full 360◦ range.
Conversely, the proposed fingertip-based method defines the navigation vector as a
pointing direction from the fingertip relative to the HMD frame origin. By decoupling the
control input from the hand or wrist’s physical orientation, the system achieves postureinvariant isotropy. Figure 4 and Table 3 illustrate the comparative results between the two
methods.

3.2. Isotropic Maneuverability Assessment via Radial Waypoint Targeting

departures from the central origin. The core significance of this test lies in its ability to
expose how control isotropy affects the operator’s capacity to navigate toward specific
sectors in space.

4.1. Integrated Discussion: Control Stability in Edge Cases and Strategic Advantages

345

Synthesizing the findings across the evaluation stages, the proposed fingertip-based
6-DOF control framework demonstrates significant potential in addressing operational
edge cases where conventional methods often face technical or ergonomic constraints.
The decision to implement a seated experimental condition was primarily driven by the
practical requirements of logistics environments, where operators must perform long-term
teleoperation of multiple agents. In such contexts, requiring an operator to constantly stand
and rotate their torso is detrimental to both user experience and health due to cumulative
physical fatigue. Furthermore, gaze-coupled methods are considered unfavorable from a
UX perspective for sustained industrial tasks, as they require operators to continuously tilt
their heads to maintain downward gaze, resulting in significant postural strain on the neck
and shoulders.
In contrast, the proposed decoupled method allows the user to navigate through subtle
finger pointing rather than physical head or body movement, thereby minimizing physical
tension while maintaining precise control. Our findings clarify that while gaze-coupled
methods might track trajectories effectively in unconstrained environments, they face inherent limitations under the seated constraints essential for long-term operation. Specifically,
Experiment 2 revealed that gaze-coupled systems are unable to facilitate viewpoint control
toward directions outside the current line of sight particularly rearward maneuvers while

proposed system achieved a 93% success rate in tasks including rearward maneuvers, providing greater accessibility than gaze-coupled methods, which faced significant ergonomic

Additionally, the proposed method maintains consistent control isotropy even at extreme operational angles. Unlike fist-based interfaces, which suffer from posture-dependent
skewness in specific sectors (particularly 4–7 o’clock) due to anatomical wrist limits, the

was further evidenced in complex sequential navigation tasks, where the system recorded a
highly precise Trajectory RMSE of 0.18. These improvements resulted in a 77.45% reduction
in TCT variance, confirming that the interface provides consistent control regardless of the
target’s spatial orientation. As a result, the proposed system leverages the HMD’s native
sensors to flexibly overcome physical and spatial constraints that conventional interfaces
have historically found difficult to address. It serves as a practical alternative that reduces
operator fatigue and maximizes operational efficiency in industrial environments where
precise, omnidirectional viewpoint transitions are essential, such as high-density logistics.
4.2. Limitations and Future Works
Despite the improved stability, the proposed framework faces inherent physical limitations under the seated condition. In a seated posture, immediate rearward rotation is
physically constrained, requiring a minimum turning radius to complete such maneuvers.
This kinematic characteristic acts as a limiting factor for ultra-precise navigation within
confined virtual environments.
Future Works will explore the generation of 3D spline paths from the current viewpoint
to a target destination. Specifically, we plan to systematically investigate the correlation
between the geometric parameters of the 3D spline and the user’s controllability. This
research direction aims to achieve UX-centered performance improvements by identifying
how precisely and intuitively operators can navigate according to their specific intentions.
4.3. Concluding Remarks
In summary, this study presented a flight-like VR locomotion framework utilizing
MLP-based fingertip gesture recognition and decoupled 6-DOF velocity mapping. By
effectively balancing control range and ergonomic stability, the proposed system provides
a robust solution for complex viewpoint control in specialized industrial teleoperation.
The experimental results demonstrated that the proposed method addresses postural
skewness and achieves a 10% reduction in Trajectory RMSE compared to the conventional fist-based method, thereby enhancing stability during complex navigation tasks.
Furthermore, the decoupled control scheme facilitated high accessibility in scenarios requiring extreme viewing angles from a seated position, where gaze-coupled methods faced
ergonomic constraints. These findings suggest that decoupled fingertip-pointing is an
efficient and ergonomic modality for omnidirectional maneuvering in high-density virtual
environments.
