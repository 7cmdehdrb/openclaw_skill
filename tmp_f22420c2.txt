Abstract
Soft robotics has attracted increasing attention as a promising alternative to rigid robots in applications where conventional rigid systems struggle, including unstructured environment exploration,
agriculture, and medical robotics. However, practical deployment remains challenging due to strong
nonlinearities, hysteresis, and effectively infinite degrees of freedom, which necessitate accurate system
modeling and control methods. Motivated by these challenges, this review provides a comprehensive analysis of soft robotics research along two principal axes: system modeling and control. System
modeling is defined as the mathematical representation of a soft robot’s instantaneous shape and
state using appropriate state variables. Existing approaches are categorized into physics-based models, model-free data-driven methods that bypass explicit physical formulations, and hybrid modeling
approaches that use physical models as a backbone while compensating for modeling inaccuracies
through data-driven techniques. Control is defined as the computation of control inputs that drive a
soft robot toward a desired shape or target tip position. Accordingly, control strategies are classified
into traditional methods such as PID control, data-driven control approaches that learn input–output
mappings, reinforcement learning–based methods that acquire control policies through interaction
with the environment, and hybrid control frameworks that integrate physical principles with datadriven methods. Through a systematic analysis of quantitative performance metrics and technological
development timelines, this review demonstrates that hybrid modeling approaches achieve state-ofthe-art performance in terms of accuracy, generalization capability, and interpretability. In the control
domain, hybrid control methods exhibit the most stable and reliable performance, while reinforcement learning–based control shows increasingly competitive results. Overall, this review elucidates
the strong interdependence between system modeling and control in soft robotics and highlights
promising directions for the development of high-performance soft robotic systems.

around two central challenges: accurate system
modeling and precise control. Figure 2 summarizes
recent research trends in soft robotics by outlining the parallel developments in system modeling
and control. In the early stage, corresponding to

coping with the strong nonlinearities characteristic of soft robotics.[1] Between 2018 and 2020,

modeling and control methods were actively introduced to address nonlinear behaviors. Attempts

become the mainstream, aiming to simultaneously

2 System Modeling
Accurate system modeling serves as a critical
link not only for precisely defining the shape and
internal states of soft robots, but also for completion of the control framework and enabling
intelligent interaction. Unlike rigid robots, which
perform geometric system modeling based on sensors, system modeling for soft robotics is defined
as a high-dimensional problem that requires the
simultaneous reconstruction of shape based on
continuum mechanics and the identification of
interaction forces with the external environment.
This section analyzes the inherent challenges and
significance of system modeling in soft robotics
and examines the technological developments that
have evolved to address these challenges. Therefore, we review representative studies within each
approach, focusing on the type of soft robot used,
the core methodologies for system modeling, and
quantitative results. A summary of this review is
provided in Table A1 in the Appendix.
The factors that make system modeling in soft
robotics challenging can be broadly summarized
into three aspects. First, kinematic uncertainty
and the infinite DoF problem. Soft robotics has
continuum structures that deform over their entire
bodies rather than discrete combinations of links
and joints.[7] As a result, it is difficult to accurately estimate the whole robot shape or the EndEffector (EEF) position using only a finite number
of embedded sensors. Second, material nonlinearity. The viscoelasticity of compliant materials

2.1.2 Continuum Mechanics &
Analytical Physics Modeling
To describe material nonlinearities that cannot be
adequately captured by purely geometric assumptions, high-fidelity modeling based on continuum
mechanics has been actively investigated. Such
approaches consistently formulate the deformations of a soft robot, including bending, torsion,
shear, and extension, based on physical principles,
and express outputs such as shape and contact
forces induced by input pressure in a predictive
mathematical form. This enables the construction of state models that retain interpretability
and are therefore suitable for use in control and
design optimization. CR theory provides a framework for soft robotics system modeling using a
centerline and associated cross-sectional frames,
allowing bending, torsion, and shear deformations
to be simultaneously considered. Grazioso et al.[2]
modeled a soft robot using CR theory in simulation and performed spatial integration through a
finite element approach on Lie groups. By leveraging Princeton benchmark experimental data, they
presented a CR-based dynamic model capable
of analyzing bending, torsion, shear, and extension. Using helical shape functions[19], the infinite
DoF of the soft robotics were reduced to a sixdimensional strain vector e(α), enabling analysis
of strain and curvature states. As a result, the
mean error (ME) of EEF error normalized by the
robot length was reported to be 0.151 ± 0.102%.

2.2.1 Embedded Sensor & Time-Series
Modeling
Unlike rigid robots, which can accurately compute
geometric states from high-precision sensor measurements, signals from embedded sensors integrated into a soft robot are difficult to exploit
for real-time system modeling.[26] This difficulty
arises because embedded soft sensors exhibit signal distortion due to time delays and nonlinearities induced by material viscoelasticity.[5, 27] In
addition, fabricating soft actuators with embedded sensors is inherently challenging, and increasing the number of sensors leads to greater wiring
complexity and signal interference, further complicating interpretation.[1, 28, 29] To address these
issues, time-series modeling approaches have been
developed that learn the time-varying characteristics of sensor signals using RNN or probabilistic modeling, enabling direct mapping without
explicit physical interpretation.

Under the growing demand for accurate system modeling of soft robotics, hybrid methodologies have emerged to complement the respective strengths and limitations of physics-based
and data-driven approaches. Specifically, physicsbased models offer high interpretability but struggle to accurately capture strong nonlinearities,
whereas data-driven models can inherently represent nonlinear behaviors but suffer from low interpretability. Hybrid system modeling approaches
address this trade-off by using physics-based models to describe the kinematic skeleton or fundamental dynamic behavior of the system, thereby
ensuring validity and interpretability. Components that are difficult to model, such as residual
dynamics, parameter uncertainties, and hysteresis, are then compensated for through neural
network learning. This strategy enables high estimation accuracy with relatively small datasets
and provides greater reliability than black-box
models, thereby attracting attention as a SOTA
technique in soft robotics system modeling. In this
section, as illustrated in Fig. 5, hybrid modeling
approaches are systematically analyzed and categorized into three sub-approaches according to
their integration strategy and objective: residual
learning and model correction, physics-informed
neural networks and structural coupling, and
filtering-based hybrid estimation.

These approaches suggest that, in soft robotics
systems where explicit system parameter identification is difficult, accurate system modeling can
be achieved using only compressed representations
of the data. While this methodology substantially improves modeling simplicity and accuracy,
it suffers from a limitation in that the physical meaning of the compressed latent variables
becomes obscure, making it difficult to provide
intuitive feedback for system design or analysis.
System modeling based on latent space representations can process data more efficiently and
simultaneously capture robot behavior compared
to the two preceding approaches. However, due
to its data-driven nature, the limitation of low
interpretability remains.

2.2.4 Data-Driven Approach :
Conclusion and Limitations
In conclusion, data-driven system modeling offers
an advantage in that it enables accurate modeling
of complex nonlinearities by learning mappings
between input and output data without explicitly
accounting for the physical complexities inherent
in soft robotics. Among the various methodologies,
time-series learning captures the temporal nonlinearities of embedded sensors, vision-based learning
exploits spatial shape information, and latent
space learning enables efficient data representation. However, black-box approaches inherently
limit interpretability, thereby hindering integration with control frameworks that rely on explicit
structural understanding. In this context, hybrid
modeling, where the results of physical models
are corrected or complemented using data-driven
techniques, has emerged as a State-of-the-Art
(SOTA) approach, as it maximizes the ability of
data-driven methods to represent the nonlinearities of soft robots while simultaneously preserving
model interpretability. This approach is discussed
in detail in Section 2.3.

In addition, there are studies that do not merely
learn residuals but instead embed physical structures into the learning process to improve data efficiency and enable real-time control. This methodology designs the neural network architecture

3.3 Control III: Reinforcement
Learning Control
The high-dimensional state space and complex
nonlinear dynamics of a soft robotics cannot be
fully addressed by traditional control, while datadriven control suffers from limitations in establishing a control model that is robust to drift errors
or variations in the characteristics of a soft robot.
As a solution to these challenges, RL, in which
a robot autonomously learns a behavior policy
that maximizes rewards through interaction with
the environment, has attracted significant attention. RL is a method that directly learns a control
policy from rewards obtained through interaction
between the robot and the environment, aiming
to acquire reward-maximizing action strategies
with data-driven appraoches rather than explicitly
defining relationships between input and output or
state transition models. When applying RL to soft
robotics control, a simulation environment with
domain randomization, as illustrated in Fig. 7, is
employed to enhance adaptation to hysteresis in
a soft robotics, and physical models such as PCC
or pre-trained models are used as the simulation
environment for training. Through this approach,
instead of ignoring or approximating aspects that
typically cause difficulties in soft robotics control, these factors are incorporated as part of the
environment during policy learning. As a result,
policies applicable under conditions involving the
absence of accurate physical models, uncertainty
in system parameters, and the presence of external
disturbances can be learned.

tracking errors of approximately 11.8–15.5 mm.

Liu et al.[53] proposed an adaptive control strategy for an opposite-bending-and-stretching structured soft robot manipulator operating in underwater environments, in which a baseline PID control is constructed and its parameters are adjusted
online through RL. In this process, Friend-orFoe Q-learning[54] was applied to autonomously
balance conflicting control objectives, effectively
overcoming disturbances and environmental perturbations caused by underwater flows. Experimental results demonstrated an improvement in
task success rate to 83.3%. Muhammad Sunny
Nazeer et al.[55] proposed a real-time adaptation strategy that combines Bayesian optimization and Gaussian process-based compensators to
alleviate performance degradation when transferring policies learned in simulation to real environments. Specifically, the dynamics of a threechamber, three-module PSA were pre-trained
using supervised learning and employed as a
simulation environment to train a PPO policy.
Subsequently, the policy output was compensated
using Bayesian optimization assisted coaching or
Gaussian process-based recurrent cerebellar architecture, enabling successful task execution. In
addition, Lanpei Li et al.[56] trained a PPO policy for in-hand manipulation that rotates grasped
objects using a four-finger pneumatic soft gripper. In this study, a policy for rotating a specific
object was pre-trained, and continual policy distillation was applied to ensure that previously
learned knowledge was retained while handling
diverse objects. As a result, a single unified policy
achieved rotation of a cubic object by up to 240◦ .

4 Conclusion and Future
Works
This paper analyzed more than 60 papers published from 2020 to the present, providing an
overview of the technological progress in system modeling and control within soft robotics.
We systematically reclassified the developmental trajectories of these two core technologies
according to the level of machine learning integration, and arranged them along a temporal
axis to identify how learning paradigms have
evolved within systems as the technology has
advanced. This approach provides meaningful
insight into the recent methodological trends
in soft robotics research. However, unlike rigid
robots, soft robotics possess continuum characteristics, and the adopted approach can fundamentally differ depending on how the system is
defined, that is, how it is represented in a low
dimensional form. Through methodological analysis, this study clarified how each work defined and
modeled a soft robotics and confirmed the corresponding meaningful performance improvements.
Nevertheless, it was limited in deriving ontological answers to why such definitions were chosen
in each study. Therefore, future research requires
a more in-depth examination of the system definition of soft robots itself.
From the perspective of technological trends,
the field of system modeling exhibits a clear tendency to converge toward hybrid models that combine the advantages of physics-based models and
purely data-driven modeling, following an evolutionary path from physical models to learningbased approaches. In contrast, while the control
field follows a similar developmental trajectory,
RL-based control strategies have also emerged as
a dominant trend. Although RL-based control still
faces a critical challenge in the form of the Simto-Real gap between simulation and real-world
deployment, many recent studies have identified
this issue as a key limitation and are actively
exploring solutions, suggesting promising future
progress in this area. In conclusion, to achieve full
autonomy and high-performance control of soft
robotics, future research should further consider
the following aspects from both system modeling
and control perspectives:
